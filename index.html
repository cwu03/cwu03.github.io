Looping Level Design in Doom Eternal

February 11, 2022 nemekkawalsky

The Doom franchise has used the concept of looping level design all the way back on Doom in 1995 and it still runs true in the latest instalment 25 years later.

I love this form of level design and wanted to dive a bit deeper into the concept and share my thoughts from a player and a design perspective.

What is Looping Level Design?

Looping level design can be described as u-shape or horseshoe level design. It’s where players have a view into a later part of the level and are teased by locations they will visit later. Players might be able to see the end goal, or just a small area which loops around on itself, but it allows players to see their goal ahead of time.

An example of this could be a burning tower in the distance which players can see, they may wonder “I hope I can reach that place, it looks cool” and as the player progresses through the level, they finally reach that tower and have that ‘Aha’ moment once they reach it.

Where appropriate it can be tied to the players objective or goal where narratively the player needs to reach that location, however it doesn’t have too. It depends how obvious you intend the design to be and sometimes subtle can be better.

Looping Level Design also works on a smaller scale too, you could design an area where players can see through a window into a later area of the level, which players will reach later on once they progress further.

Hubs also fall under this category as hubs are designed for players to return to them frequently, creating a sense of familiarity.

Below is a quick example from the second level in Doom Eternal – Exultia.

Taking a closer look at this example, players have just finished a very intense combat arena and this is a slightly calmer section which is crucial for good pacing in a level. Players are presented with the view in the image above. The way forward is on the right highlighted in green, with a nice 45 degree wall and clear ledge which stands out a bit to grab the players attention.

On the left there is a nice view of a far away area highlighted in blue, players don’t know where it is, but they know it’s a gameplay space they can see gameplay objects there. Eagled eyed players will also notice something on top of the small platform, the Slayer Gate Key, which is required to enter the gate below. There is always a small puzzle to find the key to open the door, and for the eagle eyed players who saw the key from here, know where it’s hidden.

One important thing to remember when designing an area like this, is that the player must never be confused which is the way forward. The view to the long loop should never look like the way players need to go right now.

Types of Loops

There are several types of loops, but ultimately there is no hard rule for these.

There are Long, Short, Area, Hubs and Mixed loops which combine different concepts together.

Examples of Looping Level Design

Long Loop

Near the beginning of Exultia there is a short navigation challenge, while completing the navigation players are presented with a cool view of the level and the areas to come. It actually offers both a short loop and a long loop.

Short Loop

In the example above there is a small incidental combat challenge.

  • The area is small with a bridge and a very unique asset on top where there is a key item players need to progress.
  • Players navigate under the bridge and they loop around and cross back over the bridge (highlighted in blue), looping back through this small area.

I describe this as a short loop as it only takes around 30 seconds to complete the loop, but it’s a really good use of a pretty small space.

Area Loop

Above is an example of an area loop where players loop through several times before they leave.

  • The player enters from the bottom, highlighted in green, and have a small combat section in the purple goo.
  • Looping around to the blue section where there is a simple puzzle.
  • Finally, players do some navigation to reach the orange area. Which loops around the edge of the area to where the player is in this screenshot.
  • To leave the area players need to do a final bit of navigation across the monkey bars.
Mixed – Area & Long Loop

Above is another example of a long loop, on the left side players can see off into the distance and see some intriguing looking platforms. Eagled eyed players will notice a ‘1-up’ power up below one of the platforms, which hints at how these platforms might behave, and also teases that once you reach these kinds of platforms, keep an eye out for secrets below them.

An interesting observation above which I think is coincidental but also could be an interesting hidden rule, there are 2 examples in Exultia of long loops where the way forwards is immediately to the right, both examples have subtle 45 degree geometry guiding the player to the right, while the nice view is off to the left and it’s generally far enough away that players shouldn’t feel tempted to try and access those areas right away.

Next are some examples from Level 05 – Doom Hunter Base.

The example above is from the first major combat arena. It’s subtle but there are multiple windows in the arena which into different parts of the level.

The image above is the view players have while taking a jump pad to quickly navigate from one side of the room to another. The blue and green highlighted windows look into different parts of the level.

This kind of detail is hard for players to see because players will most likely be focusing on the intense combat and not peeking through windows, but some players will notice these details, especially once the combat is over and players restock on their ammo and health scattered around the area.

In the image below I highlight the area on the 3D map.

The main strengths of these kinds of loops are actually later in the level when players are passing through those highlighted areas and look out of the windows and look back. Players are likely to remember this arena because it’s a really tough fight.

Looking a little more, there is a third area, a boss arena which can be seen highlighted in orange. A Boss room.

The boss room is the center of the whole level and a lot of the level is built around this central arena. There is a continual tease throughout the level in the build up to the boss fight, both from a looping level design perspective but also a narrative perspective as players see the Doom Hunter being constructed.

Here is a look at the 3D map and how several areas look into the boss arena, continually teasing players and foreshadowing the impending boss fight.

When to use Looping Level Design?

Not every level needs to have Looping Level Design, in fact they probably shouldn’t. If every level in your game is designed using this concept then it may feel predictable and repetitive. Looping Level Design also won’t be appropriate for all games in all genres, but if the game you’re working on could utilize this concept, then it’s another tool you as a designer have in your toolbox.

In Doom Eternal, only some levels use this concept. The ones which I would define as having Looping Level Design are shown in the image below.

Why is Looping Level Design interesting?

It’s a great way to tease what’s to come later on in the level, to foreshadow something and build up anticipation. It could be anything from a narrative moment, key objective area or even an upcoming boss fight.

Looping Level Design also creates a sense of familiarity for players, they may recognize places they have seen and the level will begin to feel familiar to them, and become more memorable. There may be a ‘Aha!’ moment where players realise they have finally reached that area they saw previously.

From a development and production perspective, it’s also a really smart way to reuse an area. Dev teams spend months or even years designing and creating levels, so the more use we get out of them, the better it is. By using part of a level as the background it can save time for artists who will have to build something there anyway, so why not use the existing level?

Of course this has to be designed very carefully, however if done correctly, it can make your level feel more grounded and believable, being able to see surrounding areas and actually being able to reach those areas in the game.

Recap

WHAT IS LOOPING LEVEL DESIGN?

  • U-Shape / Horseshoe Level Design
  • A whole level or area which loops around on itself
  • View into a later part of the level
  • Hubs

WHY IS LOOPING LEVEL DESIGN INTERESTING?

  • Teasing / Foreshadowing
  • Familiarity
  • Smart reuse of areas
  • Makes the level feel grounded / believable

TYPES OF LOOPS

  • Long
  • Short
  • Area
  • Hubs
  • Mix

Conclusion

Looping Level Design is a technique that some players will not notice while others will not.. Some players will not realise the nice views or teases designers try to create and this is okay. The goal isn’t to force this down players throats. Looping Level Design is a subtle design technique that some players will appreciate and can lead to a deeper and more memorable experience, and from a development perspective, might also save some production time by getting more value out of the great work already done by the artists, and allow players to experience it in a new way.

Looping Level Design is used in a range of games, not only the Doom series. 

  • FromSoft games such as Dark Souls, Bloodbourne, Elden Ring have great examples of Looping Level Design, often with gates, doors or ladders than can be kicked down.
  • Resident Evil series also use this technique. There are often locked doors that later can be opened up to provide players with smart shortcuts.

Tips for Looping Level Design

These are just some personal tips and advice I would suggest while trying to design a loop.

  • The loops ideally need to be planned out early in the design phase so you can get the maximum value out of them. They can also be playtested early to see if players notice and appreciate them.
  • The longer the loop the harder it is for players to appreciate because they may forget about what they saw.
  • Thematical contrast can help – If players are in an industrial area and they look out to a green field, this kind of contrast can help players remember what they previously saw.
  • When players see the loop, they should never be confused with where they should go right now, the interesting location in the distance should look exciting, but players shouldn’t believe they can reach it right away.
  • Remember the goals – What are you trying to achieve? Create a memorable experience, help save some production time? Ideally both!

This wraps up the article. I hope you have found this useful and insightful, and I hope it offers players and designers alike, something interesting to think about.

12 Guidelines for Level Design

April 26, 2018 nemekkawalsky

I have worked for over 14 years in level design, working on both multiplayer and single player games. I thought I would share some of the things I have learnt during that time which I think could be useful for other people when creating their own levels.

I focus on first person or third person shooters in a realistic environment, however many of these points could be transferred to other genres.

There are many other important elements which are not covered here, the subject of level design is very deep and I cannot list everything in one article, however, these are some of the ones I find very useful and would advise level designers to consider.

Whether you are a Level Designer, Game Designer, Artist, Producer etc, I hope you find these 12 guidelines of level design useful to give you a deeper insight into what is important for Level Designers.

Here is the quick TD;LR for those who have less time, but I highly recommend diving into the details further down the article.

GUIDELINE 1: KNOW THE GAME YOU’RE WORKING ON

I know it may sound obvious, we are making the game, of course we know it! But games evolve every day they’re in development. Developing games is an iterative process in an every day pursuit to find the fun. Designs change, decisions get made, directions change, user feedback is received.

It can be very easy to fall into a situation where you have a very solid view and understanding of your level, but a broader view of the project is also required to make sure that you’re aware of how the game is changing and evolving over time, especially if those changes have an impact on your level. This is because features may be iterated on, AI behaviour changed or added, new mechanics or gameplay tools added to the game which will impact the player experience while playing your level and you need to tweak your gameplay to accommodate those changes.

Below are 2 key areas to be aware of:

1.1: CORE GAMEPLAY

Knowing what the core gameplay of the game you’re working on is, and then see how you can build on that to create something interesting and memorable for your level. For example if there are 3 core play styles, Stealth, Assault and Ranged play styles, and the game promotes a 360 player approach with lots of player choice, how can you use those elements, along with the theme and location of the level you’re working on, to make something unique and memorable?

1.2: PLAYER NAVIGATION & ABILITIES

It’s very important to know and understand how the player can navigate and explore the world. This will give you many tools for your toolbox, for example the player can climb, jump, slide, grapple, wingsuit. Knowing this can help inspire you to create interesting navigation in your level and offer the player more choices in accomplishing their objectives.

It’s also good to consider from an early stage what the visual language is for those navigation choices is, for example all intractable objects are coloured yellow, make sure your interactive gameplay elements follow this rule. This will help you build and playtest an early blockout of your level, as the key visual languages are implemented and can be tested.

Creating a simple assault cause test map with simple geometry can showcase all of the player navigation in the game and can be a great way to inspire designers and also allow testers to check all the navigation is working as intended.

1.3: TECHNICAL GUIDELINES & METRICS

It’s imperative to understand the technical guidelines and limitations of the game engine you’re using. You do not want to create a level which will ultimately run poorly or need to be heavily optimized later on due to low performance on consoles. Learn the strengths and weaknesses of the game engine you’re using, build to the strengths and be aware of the weaknesses.

Also under the Technical Guideline umbrella are the technical requirements for player navigation, for example, how high can the player climb? Including how to avoid areas which look climbable but are just out of reach, these should be avoided to avoid player frustration. There is nothing more annoying than trying to escape near death to only find out you cannot climb up a ledge because it’s a few inches too high. There are many navigation metrics in video games, not only for the player but also the AI.

I cannot list them all here however the final one I feel is worth mentioning are combat ranges. Each weapon in the game will have different ranges. Pistols may be short range and snipers long range, as a level designer you need to consider not only what the ranges are and build your layout in accordance to this, especially cover placement, but also consider what is comfortable for players, as well as balance the difficulty. A sniper may be able to reach 100m, but is it fun to shoot enemies that far when they are merely pixels on the screen? Under some circumstances this may work well if the enemies can be easily seen, or the targets are large (a fuel truck for example) Also for the difficulty, if your level is meant to be easier or is earlier in the game, maybe the sniping range swill be shorted to make it easier.

If these guidelines for the game you’re working on are not clear, ask your lead/other colleagues, if it doesn’t exist, create a document listing all these elements and try to discuss with your lead to define them. Create a Level Design Bible for your game.

1.4: CHALLENGES

It’s good to know where the challenges come from for the player. This may include enemy AI, using different AI classes which offer different challenges, enemies with shotguns or enemies with sniper rifles offer different challenges. Then you can combine different enemy classes together to create different challenges as well as different levels of difficulty depending on the enemy composition.

There may also be Exploration challenges, where the player needs to explore and find specific things, this could be crafting materials, ammunition, collectable objects etc. While searching for these objects, the player may need to navigate different things, climb high mountains or swim inside caves. Each can offer a different flavour of challenge, along with a different difficulty and ultimately a different player experience.

1.5: UNDERSTAND THE REQUIREMENTS

Before you start working on your level it’s good to make sure you know and understand the requirements and expectations for your level. Is there an intended goal or player objective? Is there a defined theme or location for the level? Are there any specific narrative requirements or cinematics which need to take happen? How long should the level take to complete? It’s important to gather as many of these requirements before you start working, as these will help define and shape your design ideas and it can help reduce rework later on. If in doubt, ask your lead or whichever person on the team can answer these questions for you.

Keep in mind that there will almost always be changes throughout the production of a game, so it’s good to stay flexible and try and not get too attached to your ideas too early. The changes may be because of a change in direction, narrative, adapting to feature cuts etc. If and when these changes come, evaluate the change, understand the reason for the change and try to find a way to adapt and incorporate the required changes.

GUIDELINE 2: FIND AND USE REFERENCES

Reference images are a great source of inspiration, and also a great way to communicate your ideas and intentions to people. “I would like a building like this” “the area will be roughly this scale” “I am aiming for this kind of mood”.

A common mistake I have often seen people make is working on building levels without reference images, everything is from their imagination. Now depending on the game you’re working on, maybe there are no reference images easily available, for example a sci-fi game, however I am sure some can be found, or if you have access to a concept artist, they could help visualize/communicate early intentions.

A second common mistake is using references incorrectly. Sometimes people search for the perfect reference which has everything represented, the environment, lighting, mood and so on. 99% of the time it’s impossible to find an image which contains everything. It’s better to find reference images which represent each element. For example if you’re creating a ruined village, you could find references which represent the scale, another to represent the architecture style, another to represent the damage done do the buildings, even a reference image which represents the cause of why the village is in ruin like a huge fire, a battle or a plague which caused people to flee. Ultimately you can make a document or PPT with all the images on to help bring each element together.

Finding reference images:

  • Images – Google references are the obvious one and are very useful, one trick people may not know is that if you have an image of a house for example, and you would like more references of the same house, or you would like to find out where it is, you can reverse image search it on google , google will use that image as reference and search for images like that one. Just click the camera button on the google images page to either upload an image or search using the URL of an image.
  • Movies – Are a great source for references, as well as inspiration. You can capture clips or screenshots which can show a scene, structure, building, character, mood, special effects etc. Depending on the game you’re working on, there may be some reference movies to watch, for example if you’re creating a post-apocalyptic game, there may be specific post-apocalyptic movies used as the core references.
  • Games – It’s always good to know what the competition are doing and can be used as reference for not only visual or technical benchmarks, but also perhaps of things you don’t want to do. For example a game may be similar to yours, so you want to avoid specific elements to avoid looking the same. There are also many game/level design problems which other games have solved, it’s useful to see how other games solve these problems. For example what happens if the player kills the mission giver? What do we do? Other games can be looked to for reference and inspiration. Often when I play games, I take a lot of screenshots or even videos as I play to save them as references. I have almost 4,000 screenshots on steam.
  • Books – Books may be a surprising way to find references and inspiration, books can describe characters, scenarios, settings, moods very well, this can help you express and describe these elements in your design document. Even just taking a quote from a book to help express the mood or emotion you’re looking for can help a lot.
  • Music – If you are designing an action sequence, stealth sequence, emotional finale or whatever it may be, find some music which represents those scenes from your favourite from movies, tv shows, video games and listen to them while you’re designing it. Listening to action movie soundtracks while designing an action sequence can really help put you in the zone and help inspire you to create something exciting.

Remember, reference images, screenshots etc are reference and inspiration, they are not a blueprint which needs to be recreated 1:1.

GUIDELINE 3: FOCUS ON THE BROAD STROKES FIRST

It’s very easy to get lost in the details too early, this can result in losing a lot of time on things which may need to change later. If you’re building the level and adding a lot of detail, some parts of the level may feel like they are progressing faster and often too fast before proper iteration and playtesting can be done, leaving other areas of the level behind. This can make any required modifications harder and more time consuming as there is a lot more you need to change and iterate on, sometimes even resulting in scrapping it all and starting again which will result in a lot of wasted time and work.

I recommend starting with the broad strokes first and come back later to add the detail. You can do it in passes, come back and revisit areas later and add more detail later once the direction is more defined and settled. That being said it’s good to think about and plan, for example this room is going to be a canteen or a library, you can add a few key thematic props to express the intentions, or even just add text to express the theme to anyone playing.

It’s also good to sync up with the level artist at an early stage to get their input on the broad strokes, they may have valuable ideas or feedback and if they know they will need certain things, for example they need more space to work with, then you can start to work together to ensure both the design and art intentions work in harmony together.

Likewise, once the basic walkthrough of your level is playable, you can review the pacing of your level to make sure the highs and lows, intense and calm areas are well balanced so players have a chance to breath, while trying to keep them in a flow state.

Finally, you can do a technical check on the layout, checking the line of sight, view distances, transitions check between areas for loading and unloading and so on. Sync with any technical leads on the project to help highlight any areas where performance might be an issue. If your level runs at 20fps, players are not going to have fun, and worse, it could fail approval from Sony or Microsoft and delay the launch of your game! At those very late stages, the solution will often be delete, delete, delete until it runs.

So when is a good time to add detail? Once the level layout has been reviewed, approved, playtested and you know there should be no more major changes or re-designs happening, then it’s safer to add more detail.

GUIDELINE 4: SCALE AND PROPORTIONS

Make sure to continually check the scale of what you’re building to make sure it’s correct. This is especially important when doing the broad strokes during the early blockout stages, if you don’t validate the scale early, you may find yourself having built a large part of the level and realizing you need to redo it or down/upscale everything which can waste a lot of time and effort. Likewise you don’t want to build an area which is too large as the player may feel like a mouse, and it will be impossible for the art team to fill up and the final result may feel unrealistic and unbelievable. Likewise if it’s too small or narrow and built to the minimum technical guidelines, when the art team come along to make your level look beautiful, they will have no space to place any objects. Both of these cases can lead to changes later on, so the earlier the scale and proportions are clear and accurate, the easier the level production is going to be later on.

Often games use real world scale and that’s a good scale to start building to, however, this may not always be the case, so it’s good to have a concrete sense of scale for the game you’re working on. A character is a good place to start. Also if you can make your map playable as soon as possible you can run around it yourself to get a feel for the scale and proportions of what you’re creating.

Again, it’s also never too early to share ideas and discuss with the level artist who will work on the map, to share ideas and get each other’s perspectives and feedback.

GUIDELINE 5: WORK CLEAN AND SUBMIT CLEAN FILES

By working clean I mean that as much as possible you always follow the technical guidelines and submit clean work. Always follow the metrics, even from an early blockout stage. If a doorway needs to be [X]metres wide, you make sure it’s [X]metres wide! If a corridors minimum width is [X]metres, ensure all corridors are at least that wide. Additionally it’s recommended to check if you have enough space for cover, props, art detailing later on. If you’re creating an alleyway, a maintenance corridor, an office corridor etc, make sure to leave some room for the Artists to do their thing! Talk to them regularly and make sure your intentions are clear, and their intentions are clear for you.

Some of the consequences if you don’t is that when it comes to playtesting, players may get frustrated playing your level because they keep getting stuck in narrow spaces, they cannot climb areas they feel are climbable, or even the AI behave poorly because they cannot navigate the space properly. This ultimately leads to a wasted playtest and you need to now go back and clean up your work.

In the worst case, if it passes the playtest and any internal reviews, later on when the issues are discovered and changes need to be made, you may need to make drastic changes to your level which then need retesting and re-approving. For example if the AI cannot navigate well and additional flank paths need to be added, this could mean the layout needs to change a lot, which ultimately changes the way the level is played and perceived by players.

Overall it’s better to follow the guidelines from the beginning and work clean from the start as much as possible. It may take a little more time, but it will save a of headaches later on.

This also comes down to non-player facing elements, such as naming conventions, folder structures and general organisation of your work. Most elements added to a game such as the AI, patrol paths, objects etc, will have a name inside the editor. Try to name them and group them together in sensible ways. Don’t leave objects with their default name such as “Entity01” or “Object01”. Use “Area01_ObjectiveNPC_Hostage01” for example. Find a naming convention that works for not only you, but also your team. Having consistent naming conventions across all the levels in the game can be extremely useful and save a lot of time in the future. You may feel you have no time now because you’re so busy, however if you have no time now, it will be very unlikely you have more time closer to the next deadline or ship date!

Imagine a teammate of yours going away for a sunny vacation, and you have to look after their work for a while. You open up their script to fix a minor bug and you see the image below… No one will like that!

I will always remember what my 3D Studio Max lecturer said in the first lesson while at University “If you don’t use and follow naming conventions, you will get fired”.

GUIDELINE 6: ALWAYS PUT YOURSELF IN THE PLAYERS SHOES (PLAYER PSYCHOLOGY)

Building parts of your level which feel very narrow and claustrophobic, or very open, can make the player feel very differently. It may give them confidence, or take their confidence away depending on their expectations of the game. Knowing what players expect, either based on the IP you’re working on, or other expectations players may have. For example if you’re building a level for a horror game and you build a bathroom, there are some preconceptions players may have of bathrooms in the horror genre. You can use these to build tension. However try and not be too cliché!

Also as a developer, you know the game inside out, you know your level because you designed it, so it’s clear what the player needs to do. This is why it’s so important to always put yourself in the players shoes. It’s also important to put yourself in different types of players shoes. Casual players, hardcore players and everything between.

Some example things to ask yourself while wearing different shoes:

  • Is the mission objective/goal clear?
  • Does the mission objective make sense?
  • Is it clear where you need to go?
  • What elements are drawing the players attention?
  • Does everything make sense?
  • What is standing out in the environment?

    To make sure the right things are standing out in a scene – developers can do the squint test to check this. While squinting your eyes or by using a blur filter in Photoshop, what elements stand out in the scene?
  • Is the pacing of the level working okay?

If there is constant action, the player has no time to breath and have a rest to think and plan or have a sip of water. If there is nothing happening for long periods of time the player may feel bored. Finding the right pacing to keep the player feel engaged is very important. Keeping players in a flow state can be very hard to do, but can be key to keep players engaged.

  • Surprise / Memorable moments

After finishing the mission, what will the player remember? If you really have to design a fetch quest, what makes it memorable? Are you fetching something funny? Does the item get stolen and you need to retrieve it? Has the item already been taken by someone else and now you need to track them down? Games are more memorable when things surprise the player with unexpected turns of events.

Playtests are a good way to see the results, but even before that it’s good to try and anticipate this as much as possible. Once you feel things are clear for you, you can ask colleagues or people from other departments who are not familiar with the level you’re working on to play and answer some simple questions to get quick feedback. Ultimately you may have playtests with external players who are totally new to the game, these tests can limited in number, costly and time consuming, so it’s good to try and anticipate as many issues as you can, as early as you can.

GUIDELINE 7: KNOW ALL THE TOOLS IN YOUR TOOLBOX, AND USE THEM

This includes the tools used to create the level, Unreal or Unity etc, knowing these inside out and being as efficient as possible when using these tools is very important, but I would like to focus more on the design tools you have as a level designer. These are not only the gameplay features, the players navigation, abilities etc, but also specific level design tools such as:

7.1 ARCHITECTURE

Learning some basic architecture principles can be extremely useful in helping you build interesting and believable places.

A few starting points are:

  • How To Think Like An Architect: The Design Process – By Barry Berkus
  • 30×40 Design Workshop Channel– By Eric Reinholdt

Ultimately the architecture and layout of your level can influence the players decision making. If it’s an action section and there is a firefight, the player may need to find cover, if you present the player with good cover options then they will likely move there.

Likewise, going back to the horror game example, if you are being chased and there are 2 paths the player can take, stay inside a building which is narrow, or run outside where there is more open space? Now take the same example but change the lighting. The player can stay inside where it’s narrow but brightly lit, or run outside where it’s more open but it’s very dark at night. There are pros and cons and all of these elements can influence the players decision making process.

7.2 LANDMARKS

Using key landmarks to help guide the player is a very powerful and can help orient players in the game world. Landmarks can come in many sizes from large, like a lighthouse for example, to the very small, like direction of the terrain.

Naughty Dog did a great job of this when they showcased Uncharted 4 at the E3 press conference in 2015.

7.3 LIGHTING

Closely linked to player psychology is lighting, not only does lighting attract the players attention which can help guide the player the right way, if lights are wrongly placed, it can lead the player the wrong way and cause confusion. Additionally the colour and tone of the lighting can influence the players decision making. For example, warm coloured lighting vs cold coloured lighting can build a different mood and atmosphere. If faced with 2 doorways, one lit with warm and welcoming lighting vs cold blue lighting, players may likely, and possibly instinctively, be drawn to the warmly lit doorway.

An example of this is from Half Life 2. Below I have tweaked the lighting to show how lighting can influence players decision making process. There is moonlight and only 1 secondary artificial light in the scene. If the light is moved it can change totally the players understanding of where to go.

Now if all of those lights were on at the same time, the correct path would blend in with the others and it would be harder for players to find where they need to go.

7.4 SOUND

This plays an important role too, sounds can add a lot of immersion to the level and help build a believable world, whether it’s noise of farm animals, insects, generators or lights buzzing. They can also help guide the player, voices in the distance can lure the player toward enemy locations, scuttling of a rat in an air vent can draw the players attention to a stealthy side route through the ventilation system.

7.5 VISUAL LANGUAGE

This was briefly mentioned in the previous section on knowing your game, but I would like to cover it in a little more detail. Having a strong and consistent visual language is very important to make sure your level is readable by players. Often in games there will be visual languages for different gameplay elements. The most common example of this is the famous red barrel. If you see a red barrel in a shooter, it’s almost guaranteed to be an explosive barrel. Other examples can be seen in Far Cry 5, objects which can be interacted with are yellow, climbable ladders/ledges are blue. In Tomb Raider where the climbable ledges have white paint, elements which guide the player to pieces of a puzzle are highlighted in specific colours. These are subtle cues to help the player understand the environment.

Visual languages can go further and are directly linked to the player metrics. If you have a ledge which is 2-3 meters and can be climbed by players and the AI, anything from perhaps 3.1m to 5m are not allowed, this is so the player doesn’t think “Can I climb this?” The visual language need to be as clear as possible so the player is never too confused about where they can or cannot navigate.

It’s important to know what the visual language is for the game you’re working on, and make sure you follow it. The sooner the visual languages are defined, the easier playtesting the game will become!

There is nothing worse as a player than to be in an intense combat sequence and choosing to move to another position, maybe some high ground, only to find yourself jumping against a wall unable to climb when the ledge is literally at your head height, yelling at the screen “JUMMMP!!!” All while being shot in the back by the enemies and dying.

7.6 ENVIRONMENTAL STORYTELLING

Environmental Storytelling is a very powerful tool which enables you to tell many stories with little cost. It’s also open to allow players to interpret the storytelling and maybe make their own conclusions or even make their own story in their minds. This really depends on the narrative of the game and the level you’re working on. A quick example could be, we need to give the player a first aid kit, depending on the theme of the area it could be placed in a small medical station on the wall or in an open drawer, you could place a dead body on the floor and have some props along with the first aid kit to make it look like someone had tried to save their life but failed.

Weapon placement example

Another quick example, if you have a mission where you simply need to kill all the hostile enemies in the area, a way to add more interest and storytelling to this could be to have the enemies doing something meaningful, they could be searching the area high and low. All the desks are messy, drawers opened, papers on the floor, cabinets open, beds have been overturned. The player will being to wonder what they are searching for? Why are they searching for it? Depending on the game you’re working on, you may need to discuss this with the narrative team, level artists etc to make sure the direction is okay with them.

7.7 CONTINUAL LEARNING

A great tool is your ability to keep learning. There are always new things to learn and experience. Try and always be open minded and hungry to learn more about level design. Talk to peers, research online, check out GDC talks, Game Makers Toolkit, NoClip and many more great resources out there.

Your learning potential doesn’t need to be limited to only level design too. You can extend your knowledge to other game development fields too. Learning the basics of environment art, audio design, programming, game design, project management. All these skills, even the basics, will help you understand the needs and requirements from the other people working alongside who help you to realize your level and bring it to fruition.

Finally, having a desire to learn more about the real world, history, architecture, nature etc can really help give you more ideas and references which can help inspire your level designs.

There are many more, maybe next time.

GUIDELINE 8: STAGING

Staging key parts of your level is very important. If you take a look at the key areas of a level, these are usually the start point, the quest giver, the objective area, and the final ending etc. These areas are more important than other areas where nothing special or specific happens. As a level designer you may need to trigger cinematics at the beginning of the level, trigger set pieces or cinematics near the objective area. Even with no cinematics the objective area, for example where a hostage is or where a bomb is placed, this area needs to be presented well to the player. The player needs to easily understand the challenge and the objective.

The staging, composition and overall way the area is revealed to the player is crucial. A good example of this is Dark Souls, when you enter Anor Londo for the first time and you are presented with a really impressive view of this area with the objective clearly in the distance, this is really a great wow moment that keeps players wanting to play more.

In linear games this is easier as you know where the player will come from, in open worlds it’s more challenging as the player may be able to come from any direction. However, you can influence the player a little as you know the direction from the quest giver to the mission location, most players will arrive from the East for example. Or you can possibly breadcrumb certain things to help lead the player. If the player needs to search a house for a key to enter the town hall, you know the player will need to search the house first, then upon leaving the house, the town hall could be in the players view and the path to it has a nice reveal and composition. In this example, the town hall could be the key landmark to help guide the player as it’s an important building in the town, it’s impressive to look at and usually is in an important part of the town. Overall this landmark is very useful to help players orientate themselves, and the way it’s staged and presented to the player is very important.

GUIDELINE 9: ITERATE, ITERATE, ITERATE!

Iteration is very important when designing and building level blockouts. A good tip is to look at the time you have to deliver the design and try and plan your time to allow multiple iterations. The first idea you have for a level may not be the best one. I try to recommend people do 3 versions of a layout if you can, then review, discuss, and maybe even play each one and get feedback. You may end up merging different ideas together to get the best result which is the most fun to play.

For example if you have 1 week to deliver a level design document, you may need 1 day for research, 3 days for the design/layout and 1 day for the actual documentation and presentation of the level. The goal is to deliver at least one solid level design, so depending on your experience, work speed, level size etc, for the 3 days on design, you could allocate half a day to a layout (1.5d) half a day to review/ponder/feedback. And then 1 day to possibly merge or iterate on one of the layouts to create.

Some places to start when trying a different version of a layout could be to use different references, different layout, more or less height variation, different navigation flow through the area, place the objective in different locations to see how it could change the gameplay, for better or for worse, you don’t know until you have tried!

Overall you need to manage your time well to ensure you still deliver one solid design on time. However the more experience you get and the more you know the engine/tools and the game you’re working on, the faster and more efficient you will become at iterating and trying new ideas.

GUIDELINE 10: TO BUILD A BELIEVABLE WORLD, EVERYTHING NEEDS TO HAVE A PURPOSE

A common mistake of new level designers and environment artists is to place things in the world which make no sense. This applies to large structures and right down to the small details. For example if you have a level set in an abandoned mine in the middle of the desert, hundreds of miles from civilization and you place a box of freshly picked fruit and vegetables as a cover object, this may not make a lot of sense and it will feel out of place and surreal. Why are there so many boxes in the middle of nowhere? And why are they filled with fruit? It’s very important when trying to build a believable world that everything makes sense in the context the game. Everything you add in the world needs to have a purpose. This will lead players to believing in the world you are trying to create.

Depending on what you’re making, you can ask yourself different questions, for example, when designing and adding anything into the level you could ask yourself:

“In the game world/narrative…”

  • Who built it? Who built the building or who placed the objects there? (People lived there? working there?)
  • What was the function/purpose of the building? Was it a living space? An office? A castle? Or if you’re placing props, what was the use of the props? 
  • Why was the building built there? Or why did someone place the object there?
  • When was it built, is it an old building or more modern? When were the objects placed there? Was it recently or has it been there for many years?
  • How was the building built? How were the materials sourced, or how did the prop end up being where it is, was it placed or did it fall there due someone or something knocking it over?

If you have good answers to all of those questions, then I believe you’re going in the right direction, and if anyone questions “Why is this box here?” There is some world logic to justify why it’s placed there. The answer should never be “It’s there because I needed some stuff”

Another important element is to think about what the NPCs (non-playable characters) are doing in the world. They need to have a sense of purpose. There is nothing worse than seeing NPCs in a level stood still waiting to be killed.

It’s good to try and give the NPCs an agenda. Red Dead Redemption 2 did a great job of this, where NPCs have a full agenda, you can follow them around all day and see them do different tasks, work, eat, relax, drink in a bar, sleep. This really makes the world feel more believable because the characters in it are doing things which make sense in the context of the game.

Try putting yourself in the shoes of the characters in the game, what would they be doing? What are their needs? This is a great opportunity to build upon characters too, perhaps there is a character who is hiding something and if the player follows that character around the level, that NPC can be seen doing something suspicious, or hiding something in the environment. This can add a lot of cool unique experiences for players.

Finally, you may be asking, what if the game you’re working on is a non-realistic game? In those cases where you are building a world which isn’t meant to make sense, or is very simplistic, then there is more freedom, however some players will still question the world logic and if you can tell a story behind your world, players will find it much more depth in the game.

GUIDELINE 11: COMMUNICATION

Having good communication skills is a great asset. You could have the best idea ever but if you’re unable to communicate it to anyone, the idea will likely never become anything and great opportunities will be missed. I highly recommend everyone learn and improve their presentation and communication skills to enable them to communicate their ideas clearly and efficiently. This is not only useful when communicating with your level artist, but also fellow level designers, team leads, directors, QC, marketing or anyone that needs to know about your level. Having efficient communication and presentation skills will be extremely useful.

Some tips for this would be:

  • Know the target audience – Knowing your audience and understanding what information is important to them or what information they need to know is very important. If you’re presenting ideas to other level designers, the content will be tailored towards them. If you’re briefing the programmers on your level needs, you may need to tailor your presentation towards what’s important for them. You don’t want to lose them in lots of specific level design topics which are perhaps not useful to them, and we all know the time of programmers is very valuable!

Likewise if you’re presenting the ideas to your lead / director who will approve them, they may need to know specific things to help them validate the idea. If you’re not sure who your target audience is, or what information is important to them, you can ask them before hand, “Is there anything specific you would like me to include?” and then you can prepare specific answers/examples for them.

  • Presenting ideas– If you’re presenting a design idea, think about the goal of the presentation, what do you want people to leave the room remembering? What are the key takeaways? It’s linked to the point about knowing the audience, but it also depends on what you are presenting. Is it a level design document to a team of people? Is it an idea you have and you are trying to communicate and present the idea to your lead who will validate it? Make sure you’re clear on what you are trying to present, and make sure the goal is clear. The content and tone of delivery may be very different.

Let’s use an example, you have an idea you think is great and your goal is to present the idea to your lead and hopefully convince them it’s a great idea and have them approve it. Try to find a way to keep it simple and concise, but with all the key information they need to validate the idea. What information is important to your lead? What will help sell the idea? What information does your lead need to approve the idea? If you know your lead well, what questions do they usually ask or what do they look for when people commonly propose ideas?

I recommend to focus on the player experience and why your idea will result in a better player experience and a better game overall, and your idea/change is worth the investment. Show that you have thought about it from all aspects, from not only gameplay but a production perspective. Will it save time? Will it add time? Does it have a good return on investment? If so, how much? Try to present the idea with the key information required for your lead to be able to make a decision. Remember your leads time is valuable too, so you want to make sure when you present your idea, it’s very clear and well thought out.

With time and practice, you will become more efficient at this. I recommend you present ideas to fellow level designers first and try and convince them, if they find flaws in your design, you can find solutions and ensure you have a solid argument before presenting to your lead.

GUIDELINE 12: PLAY YOUR LEVEL, AND THE GAME REGULARLY

Overall the best advice is to play your level a lot, play, iterate, play, iterate.

Too often do I hear the phrase “But it works fine in the editor”, but when playing the game in the full build of the game, there are issues. Sometimes playing it in the editor will have differences to the fully compiled build of the game. The build is the only version that really matters as that is what will be played by real players. So try and play a built version of your game whenever possible.

I recommend playing through your level every day before you start working, this gives you good visibility on the current status and health of your level. If there are any glaring issues, you can highlight them and this may influence the priority of the tasks you work on that day.

Also thoroughly test all the changes you make, no matter how small they are is recommended. Jump in the game run around and test the changes properly. This can help maintain a healthy build.

It’s also good to play other levels in the game you’re working on. Get to know the game and what other content is being created. Your level needs to feel consistent with the game vision, to ensure this, you need to see how the whole game is progressing. A good way to achieve this is by trying to allocate a specific time slot each week to play other levels in the game. Have a game session with other people and share feedback and ideas.

While playing the game, try and play in different ways, use different weapons, use different play styles and approaches, try to play casually, play hardcore. Know your level inside out and try to understand how players may play your level. This will make the playtests more useful as you will have already covered some of the bases, however one thing I can guarantee is that when you watch new players playtest your level, you will be surprised by the way some players play which you would never have imagined!

CLOSING

In closing, I hope you found these guidelines useful. Here is a recap:

  • Guideline 1: Know the game you’re working on – The game is always changing and evolving. You need to keep up-to-date with the changes, especially those that might impact your level.
  • Guideline 2: Find and use references – These are key to help communicate your intentions with the rest of the team, and help you build a grounded and believable world.
  • Guideline 3: Focus on the broad strokes first – Don’t add too many details too early, it will be harder to iterate and you will only lose time as you iterate and make changes to find the fun.
  • Guideline 4: Scale and proportions – Always check these early to make sure you’re building to the right scale! This will save you a lot of time and effort.
  • Guideline 5: Work clean and submit clean files – This is crucial for collaborative team work. Making levels and games is a team effort. If people need to touch your work or help fix something, it’s only respectful to keep your work neat and tidy!
  • Guideline 6: Always put yourself in the players shoes (Player Psychology) – We are making games for players, so always try to put yourself in their shoes. See what they see. It’s very easy to forget and keep looking at things from a god-like view as a developer.
  • Guideline 7: Know the tools in your toolbox, and use them – There are so many great tools level designers can use from landmarks to lighting and more. Learn about these tools and put them to use to make your levels stronger.
  • Guideline 8: Staging – All first impressions are important, right? Presenting the player with your level for the first time, that first view, the money shot, players should feel impressed when they reach your level and not want to put the controller down.
  • Guideline 9: Iterate, iterate, iterate! – Ideas you have on paper may not work out once they are playable, so the first idea you have will never be the final idea in the game. This is normal. Iterating on your ideas is key to finding the fun and making sure you create the best experience for players, which really compliment the game design and vision of the game.
  • Guideline 10: To build a believable level, everything needs to have a purpose – Who, What, Why, When How are key questions to ask with everything you add to the level. This will help lead to creating a world which players can believe in, that don’t feel random.
  • Guideline 11: Communication – It’s better to over communicate that not communicate enough. Games can change very fast as they are iterate on and if a team is not in sync with each other and all aware of what’s happening, it can lead to a lot of wasted time and effort. Every team member should be on the same page and working in the same direction with the same goals.
  • Guideline 12: Play your level, and game regularly – What is in the game build is what your project leads will see and ultimately what players will see, it’s imperative to regularly play that version to see the health of your level, before it gets in the hands of others.

As mentioned earlier there are many more elements to consider as a level designer and I could talk about it for hours and hours. I feel these were some of the more important ones I wanted to share with you today.

A final closing tip I would like to give is to always be curious, always be hungry to learn and be proactive in everything you do, and always keep an open mind.

Good luck to you all in the future.
